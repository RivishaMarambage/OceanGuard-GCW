Sustainable Fishing Practices

Balancing global food demands with the long-term survival of our marine ecosystems.

Principle of Sustainable Harvesting

Sustainable fishing is an act of harvesting seafood in a way that makes the fish population strong and fruitful in the long run.

It is more than just the collection of fish that takes place today, but it is a requirement to retain enough biomass to allow the species to be self-renewable.

By following the bio limits of the sea ecosystems we would be preventing the extinction of complete species and thus protecting the livelihoods of the millions of people who depend on the sea as a source of food and jobs.

Conserving the Seafloor and Marines

The way we fish is just as important as the amount of fish we are taking. Many of the commercial methods are comparable to clear-cutting a forest; they use large machines to drag the ocean floor, thus destroying coral reefs and ocean seagrass fields that are essential nurseries of marine animals.

Sustainable practices focus on low-impact equipment, use pole-and-line or traps, which focus on a particular species only without causing harm to the surrounding. The conservation of these habitats would ensure the marine ecosystem is strong and rich.

Minimizing Bycatch Effects

One of the issues in fisheries today is bycatch - the unintentional fishing of non-target aquatic creatures, including dolphins, turtles, and immature fish.

These animals are also dumped into the sea in unsustainable practices, most of the time dead or dying. To significantly reduce such occurrences, sustainable fisheries use the latest technology to apply various solutions, such as Turtle Excluder Devices (TEDs) and altered hooks.

We can preserve marine biodiversity and avoid the unintentional extinction of endangered species as a collateral effect of food production by employing more discriminating practices.

Empowering the Conscious Consumer

We as individuals have a lot of power in the decisions that we make at the supermarkets and restaurants. When we purchase products bearing the mark of a certified sustainable (e.g., a blue MSC checkmark) we reward fisheries that act responsibly.

The ability to vary our diet to embrace less well-known, prolific species instead of mostly depending on so-called top predator fish like tuna will relieve congestion on the weak stocks. Small steps that the students and citizens are taking would guide the worldwide industry towards a more moral and sustainable future of our blue planet.
