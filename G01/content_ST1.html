The Problem of Plastic Pollution

Every year, 8 to 12 million metric tonnes of plastic waste enters our oceans, affecting ecosystems from the surface to the deepest trenches.

Single-use plastics like bags, bottles, and straws are used for minutes but persist for hundreds of years. Once in the ocean, they break into microplastics that contaminate the entire marine food web.

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch covers an area twice the size of Texas. Yet most ocean plastic is invisible — sunk to the seafloor or broken into microscopic particles.

Solving this crisis requires individual behaviour change, community action, corporate responsibility, and government policy working together.

How It Affects Marine Life

Over 700 marine species interact with plastic debris through ingestion or entanglement.

Sea turtles mistake plastic bags for jellyfish. Over 50% of all sea turtles have ingested plastic, causing digestive blockages and starvation.

Seabirds like albatrosses feed floating plastic to their chicks, filling their stomachs with indigestible material.

Microplastics consumed by plankton accumulate up the food chain through bioaccumulation, reaching predators and human food sources.

Coral reefs suffer when plastic smothers coral structures, increasing disease risk by up to 89%.

Actions Students Can Take

Students can make a real difference. Here are practical actions:

  • Refuse single-use plastics: Carry reusable bottles, bags, and cutlery.
  • Organise campus cleanups: Start environmental student societies.
  • Educate peers: Share information on social media and in presentations.
  • Support sustainable brands: Choose minimal or compostable packaging.
  • Advocate for policy: Support legislation restricting single-use plastics.
  • Join citizen science: Monitor and record marine debris data.

Ocean Cleanup Innovations

Innovative technologies are removing existing ocean pollution:

The Ocean Cleanup Project uses passive collection systems driven by ocean currents to gather floating debris at scale.

Seabin Project installs floating rubbish bins in harbours that filter trash, microplastics, and oil — capturing up to 1.4 tonnes per year each.

Mr. Trash Wheel — a solar and water-powered device at river mouths — has collected over 1,600 tonnes of rubbish since 2014.

Biodegradable alternatives made from seaweed and mushroom mycelium can replace traditional plastics and break down harmlessly.

Experts agree: the most effective solution remains reducing plastic at the source.
